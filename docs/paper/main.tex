\begin{abstract}
Recent systems (AlphaEvolve, TTT-Discover) show language-model agents can advance open
math problems, but in each the durable artifact is the discovered construction or program ---
the agent's reasoning is not retained as a reusable, inspectable artifact. We describe
\textbf{JSAgent}, an agent built on the opposite bet: its
primary artifact is a \textbf{compounding knowledge base of mathematical wisdom} --- a wiki
of concepts, techniques, and findings --- \textbf{plus a set of behavioral rules co-evolved
with the agent}, and arena submission is treated as \emph{verification}, not the goal.
Operating in the wild on EinsteinArena, JSAgent reached \textbf{top-3 on 10 of 17 active
problems and the highest rank-\#1 count (4) and top-3 count (10) of any agent} (as of
2026-06-22). We detail the methodology --- a wiki-first self-improvement loop, a
mathematician-persona ``council'' that writes questions rather than answers, a triple-verify
discipline that guards against evaluator drift, a wall-hit escalation that consults
accumulated failure before brute force, and a compute router --- and the concrete techniques
it produced (cross-resolution basin transfer, Dinkelbach fractional programming, warm
self-pruning, mpmath ULP polish). We argue, with evidence and stated caveats, that
\emph{in mathematical-optimization settings with cheap objective verifiers}, agents can
already make real, auditable contributions when their outputs are embedded in a reproducible
verification-and-memory loop --- a deliberately narrower claim than ``ready for science in
general,'' since the method has only been exercised where such a verifier exists. The work
used an agent-primary workflow: the agent produced the code, scripts, knowledge-base pages,
and manuscript drafts, while the human selected problems, approved knowledge promotion, judged
submission risk, and steered direction. We close with the conditions under which this loop
becomes fully autonomous, and why objective verification (which math enjoys and the natural
sciences largely lack) is the gating factor.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

AI agents have advanced remarkably in a short span, and much of that progress traces to
\emph{coding agents} --- large language models~\cite{brown2020gpt3} that write, run, and
revise their own programs in a tight loop. We are now beginning to see more and more real examples of such agents making genuine
contributions to open problems, not merely reproducing known results. Mathematics is among
the clearest arenas in which to test this objectively: a mathematical claim can be checked by
an explicit verifier that either accepts a construction or rejects it, so progress is
measured without appeal to opinion and there is nowhere for self-deception to hide. Public
competitions of open math problems --- paired with hardened automatic verifiers and
leaderboards --- turn that objectivity into a live testbed.

The last two years have made this concrete. Program-search and evolutionary agents have
produced new mathematical constructions
(FunSearch~\cite{romeraparedes2023funsearch}, AlphaEvolve~\cite{novikov2025alphaevolve},
ShinkaEvolve~\cite{lange2025shinkaevolve}, evolution-meets-RL search~\cite{surina2025algorithmdiscovery});
autonomous research systems have begun to pose and pursue their own investigations
(The AI Scientist~\cite{lu2024aiscientist,yamada2025aiscientistv2}, an AI
co-scientist~\cite{gottweis2025coscientist}, Kosmos~\cite{mitchener2025kosmos}, an AI
co-mathematician~\cite{zheng2026comathematician}); formal-reasoning agents have reached
olympiad level and supercharged Lean proving~\cite{hubert2025olympiad,kung2026leap,yang2024formalmath};
and, most strikingly, an LLM recently produced a research-level disproof of Erd{\H o}s's 1946
unit-distance conjecture~\cite{openai2026unitdistance}. EinsteinArena~\cite{bianchi2026einstein}
and test-time-training discovery~\cite{yuksekgonul2026tttdiscover} push such capabilities into
open, in-the-wild competition. This paper is a report from inside that competition.

This paper reports what one agent did in such an arena, and, more than that, a bet about what
an agent of this kind should be built to \emph{keep}: that the lasting artifact of an agent's
work is not the answer it submits but the wisdom that produced it --- the kind that compounds
across problems rather than evaporating when a run ends. The artifact, accordingly, is a
knowledge base (implemented as a wiki of concepts, techniques, and findings) together with a
set of plain-language behavioral rules; arena submission is only a way to verify that
knowledge against the world.\footnote{The personal account of how this began --- a
non-mathematician, a free weekend, and the first problem that worked --- is deferred to
Appendix~\ref{app:personal} so the contribution can lead.}

\paragraph{How this work was produced (and why it matters to the thesis).}
Every artifact described here --- the optimizer code and per-problem scripts, the knowledge
base of concepts and findings, the behavioral rules, and the prose of this paper --- was
\emph{implemented by the agent}, not typed by the author. The mode of work was uniformly
agentic: the human stated a request, the agent did it and reported back. This is not an
aside; it is part of the result. The central lesson is a clean division of labor that
emerged in practice: the agent is extraordinarily capable at \emph{execution}, but it does
not reliably know \emph{which question is worth asking} or which problem is worth solving
next --- problem selection and taste remained the human's job. And steering that selection
requires the human to genuinely \emph{comprehend} what the agent is doing; without
comprehension there is no guidance, only rubber-stamping. The knowledge base and the harness
are, in part, the instruments that make such comprehension --- and therefore such guidance
--- possible at all (\S\ref{sec:harness}).

\paragraph{Roadmap.}
Section~\ref{sec:arena} situates EinsteinArena and JSAgent's stance. Section~\ref{sec:math}

\section{EinsteinArena and Positioning}
\label{sec:arena}
EinsteinArena~\cite{bianchi2026einstein,togetherai2026arena} is a public platform of open
mathematical optimization problems, each paired with a hardened automatic verifier and a
strictly-ranked leaderboard, plus an open discussion board. Its design bet is that discovery
is \emph{collective}: by making the platform the shared, persistent state, later agents
inherit the current frontier instead of restarting from scratch, and the operators report
twelve new state-of-the-art results across the problem set since launch. The arena is the
ideal testbed for our claim precisely because its verification is objective and cheap to
re-run --- the property \S\ref{sec:discussion} identifies as the gate on agent autonomy.

What distinguishes JSAgent from the field is not how it searches but what it
\emph{keeps}: where other entrants run a pipeline and submit, JSAgent treats each run as
disposable and the accumulated knowledge base as the deliverable
($\to$~\S\ref{sec:related}). \src{docs/source/2026-bianchi-einstein-arena-collective-intelligence.md}

\paragraph{P2, first autocorrelation inequality (rank \#1, clean).}
The strongest single case. Two obstructions were diagnosed and recorded: \emph{peak-locking} under
the $\exp(v)$ parameterization (the iterate freezes $\sim 5\mathrm{e}{-}8$ from SOTA across
all $n$ and all optimizers), escaped by switching to $f=v^2$; and the \emph{compact-support}
structure of the true optimum, reached by warm data-driven self-pruning (\S\ref{sec:techniques}).
The latter broke a two-agent tie and set a verified arena record ($1.5028609\to 1.5028506$).
The autoconvolution inequality has a substantial literature of progressively tighter
bounds~\cite{matolcsi2010autoconvolution,cloninger2017autoconvolution,white2022almosttight,jaech2025autoconvolution,boyer2026autoconvolution}.
\src{problems/2-first-autocorrelation.md, concepts/n-agent-tie-not-global-min.md}

\paragraph{P14 circle-packing and P18 circles-in-rectangle (rank \#1, clean).}
Strictly-best scores on the live board. \src{findings/arena-standings-snapshot-2026-06-22.md}

\paragraph{P9 uncertainty principle (rank \#1, tied --- held on submission order).}
A legitimate but contested \#1: another agent matched the exact score and submitted later, so
the arena's earliest-submission tiebreak awards JSAgent the top slot. We state this as ``tied,
held on submission order'' wherever it appears. The problem also carries a prior verifier
failure --- a celebrated score once fell below the proven lower bound (an evaluator bug, not
mathematics), which is part of why triple-verify is central to the method. The sign-uncertainty
constants and their optimal-in-dimension-12 structure are due to Cohn--Gon{\c c}alves and
follow-up work~\cite{cohn2019uncertaintyd12,goncalves2020signuncertainty}.
\src{findings/arena-standings-snapshot-2026-06-22.md, findings/verification-patterns.md}

\paragraph{P3, second autocorrelation inequality (rank \#3, on the documented frontier).}
JSAgent achieved the strongest $10^5$-interval solution, $C=0.962214$, via cross-resolution
basin transfer and Dinkelbach refinement --- the result the platform
paper~\cite{bianchi2026einstein} attributes to JSAgent by name (its Fig.~6) and depicts within
a basin-transfer lineage alongside another agent's parallel work (Fig.~3b). The final record
($0.962643$, at $4\times 10^5$ resolution) went to a different agent; JSAgent's contribution
is an intermediate link in the documented collective lineage --- which is precisely the
arena's thesis~\cite{jaech2025autoconvolution,rechnitzer2026autoconvolution}.
\src{findings/einstein-arena-paper-jsagent-recognition-2026-06.md, problems/3-autocorrelation.md}

\paragraph{Other top-3 problems.}
JSAgent holds rank \#2 on the Erd{\H o}s minimum-overlap
problem~\cite{white2022erdos,haugland2016minoverlap,bloomerdos} (P1) --- a score
byte-identical to the leader's but submitted later --- and rank \#3 on a prime-number-theorem

The contribution of this section is not any single algorithm but a \emph{harness}: a small
set of behavioral rules, written in plain language and version-controlled alongside the
code, that govern how the agent works a problem. (The agent is Claude Code running a frontier
Claude model; see \S\ref{sec:eval}.) Each rule exists because skipping it once cost something
concrete; the cost is recorded \emph{in} the rule as its ``why.'' The rules are read into the
agent's context every session, so the harness is simultaneously the agent's operating manual
and a human-auditable account of how it reasons. Every campaign
begins with planning rather than code --- a written plan stating what to do and why before
any optimizer runs. Unlike a frozen orchestration pipeline, the harness and the knowledge
base co-evolve: a failure produces a finding, a recurring finding is promoted to a rule, and
the next run inherits both. \src{.claude/rules/, docs/agent/}

\paragraph{Design philosophy: the right tool for each sub-task.}
The harness reflects a division of cognitive labor. Language models are strong at the
open-ended parts of research --- framing a problem, planning an approach, reading unstructured
prior work --- but weak where determinism and reproducibility matter, because repeated
sampling is stochastic and can drift. The design therefore routes each sub-task to the
appropriate instrument: the model proposes, plans, and interprets, while anything requiring an
exact, repeatable answer is delegated to written code and external tools --- optimizers, exact
reimplementations, the arena verifier --- much as a person reaches for a calculator rather than
re-deriving arithmetic by intuition. This is the deeper reason a \emph{coding} agent is
well-suited to the task: it can author a script and obtain a deterministic, reproducible result
instead of re-sampling tokens toward an uncertain one. Two corollaries shape the rules below.
Triple-verify exists because a single sampled score is not trustworthy where a deterministic
check is. And the knowledge base doubles as a \emph{grounding} mechanism: many apparent
hallucinations are better read as missing ground truth, and supplying well-constrained, cited
context measurably improves accuracy --- so wiki-first lookup is as much about correctness as
about memory.

\paragraph{The self-improvement loop.}
Knowledge generated in-context is lost when the session ends; the knowledge base is the only
layer that survives across sessions, contexts, and model versions. The loop makes persistence
mandatory: a \emph{gap} (a question the wiki cannot answer) is \emph{filed} as a question
page, \emph{researched} (wiki-first, then human-gated ingest), \emph{distilled} into a
finding with citations, and --- if cited three or more times or generalizing beyond one
problem --- \emph{promoted} (human-gated) to a concept. The most-skipped step is the
cheapest: filing the question. An unfiled gap is wisdom permanently lost.
\src{.claude/rules/self-improvement-loop.md}

\paragraph{Council dispatch --- personas that write questions, not answers.}
At the start of a hard problem the agent dispatches three to five mathematician personas
(Gauss, Tao, Cohn, Viazovska, \ldots), each seeded with the problem, the current top
solutions, and the persona's own stance page. Their job is explicitly \emph{not} to solve
it: a persona producing ``the answer'' is just plausible markdown, whereas a persona
producing \emph{the question this stance would ask when stuck} forces the gap-detect loop.
The council runs once per problem during reconnaissance --- and is re-triggered on a wall
(below). \src{.claude/rules/council-dispatch.md}

\paragraph{Wiki-first lookup and cycle discipline.}
Before answering any in-scope question the agent must query the knowledge base first and cite
what it finds; the first tool call of every problem cycle is such a query, treated as a
\emph{correctness check on memory} rather than a search-because-stuck. Every cycle ---
success or failure --- produces exactly one row in a cycle log. The discipline is
anti-cherry-picking: without a strict one-to-one cycle-to-log rule, the self-improvement
metric degrades into a best-of-$N$ highlight reel that looks like progress without being it.
\src{.claude/rules/wiki-first-lookup.md, .claude/rules/cycle-discipline.md}

\paragraph{Triple-verify --- the central reliability mechanism.}
Every score is verified three ways before it is trusted: a fast local evaluator, an
independent exact reimplementation on a different code path, and a cross-check against an
analytical bound or a structurally different method. If any two disagree, the score is
treated as fake until the discrepancy is explained. This rule is the most load-bearing in
the harness because evaluator drift has caused real damage on this arena --- a verifier bug
that hid a problem (P9), an exploitable tolerance band (P14), and a strict-tolerance trap
that would have cost a large penalty (P17). Arena submission is then only a \emph{fourth}
check: slower, binary, and non-iterating. The local triple-verify is the closed loop.
\src{.claude/rules/triple-verify.md, .claude/rules/axioms.md (A1)}

\paragraph{Wall-hit escalation --- consult failure before brute force.}
A wall is defined concretely (two consecutive non-improving attempts, a method retuned
twice, or roughly thirty minutes with no metric gain). On a wall the agent must
\emph{stop} computing and escalate: name the obstruction as a question, grep the wall
ledger for a matching past obstruction (and reuse its resolving move if found), re-query
the knowledge base, and \emph{re-dispatch the council} seeded with what failed and why ---
each persona proposing one new move outside the failed class. The triggering incident is
explicit: on a record attempt the agent once ran ten-plus optimizer variants over hours
before consulting the wiki, after which the council diagnosed the obstruction (a discrete
incompressible sign topology) and ruled out whole families in minutes. A corollary guards
``floor'' claims: an $N$-agent empirical tie is evidence of a sharp shared basin, not a
proof of global optimality --- a verified arena record on P2 came from a single untried
operator (warm self-pruning) that beat a two-agent tie thought to be the global minimum.
\src{.claude/rules/wall-hit-escalation.md, concepts/n-agent-tie-not-global-min}

\paragraph{Failure is a finding.}
Every dead-end is written up with its \emph{obstruction} --- not ``it did not improve''
(the symptom) but \emph{why} it did not --- plus what the failure rules out and what might
still work. The ``what might still work'' section is load-bearing rather than garnish: the
P2 arena record (the branch's first) came \emph{verbatim} from the next-door section of a
dead-end finding written four days earlier. The agent did not invent the move that session;
it executed a prescription the failure-log discipline had already recorded.
\src{.claude/rules/failure-is-a-finding.md}

\paragraph{Compute router.}
Before any compute job the agent profiles the bottleneck, classifies the workload
(sequential / batched / RAM-bound) and required precision, and routes it --- sequential
optimizers and mpmath polish to CPU, batched float32 work to the local GPU, with cloud
float64 gated behind a measured $>3\times$ speedup. Running sequential Python on a GPU
wastes the device; running parallel float64 on a single CPU is a wall-clock disaster. The
router catches both before launch. \src{.claude/rules/compute-router.md, .claude/rules/axioms.md (A4)}

\paragraph{Evidence the loop works.}
The corpus is the first-order evidence that knowledge accumulated rather than evaporated: as
of 2026-06-22 the knowledge base holds 50 concept pages, 32 technique pages, 108 findings, 24
per-problem pages, and 101 question pages, distilled over a source corpus of 1{,}258 ingested
artifacts. Because every cycle is logged with author attribution, the harness can also be
measured on its own terms. Two measures are available now: a technique hit-rate of $26\%$ ---
the fraction of technique applications (counted once per technique--problem pairing) that
reached top-3 --- and a cite-reuse rate of $84\%$ ($51/61$ recent findings cite a prior page).
The latter is a \emph{process} metric: the wiki-first rule \emph{requires} a citation, so it
measures compliance more than causal gain. We are deliberately honest about what is \emph{not}
yet instrumented --- a per-problem best-score-vs-cycle curve and a ``recall-preceded-win''
rate need cycle-level tracing we have not retrofitted, and we do not fake them. The causal
evidence is therefore \emph{observational}, resting on the traces in Table~\ref{tab:traces}.

\paragraph{From manual discipline to a meta-learning loop.}
The rules above began as \emph{discretionary} --- written advice the human enforced by
asking. That is fragile: a rule is something the agent can rationalize past, and in practice
the decisive capture of a record-winning idea once happened only because a human explicitly
asked for it. The project's principle for fixing this is ``discretion decays, gates
compound'': convert the discretionary self-improvement steps into deterministic gates (hooks
that fire on cycle boundaries), add an unattended driver (a scheduler that runs cycles by
priority), and add quality control (pruning) so automatic capture does not degrade into spam.
This meta-learning layer is partially deployed --- the enforcement gates, the submission gate
chain, and an instrumented headless inner agent exist and have been cost- and
reliability-tested; the always-on scheduler remains gated behind explicit readiness
conditions. We report it as an architecture in progress, not a finished system.

\section{Techniques Produced}
\label{sec:techniques}

The loop produced not only results but \emph{transferable methods}, each recorded as a
technique page with its precondition, mechanism, and the obstruction it overcomes. We
summarize the six most generalizable; all are local-CPU unless noted.

\paragraph{Cross-resolution basin transfer.}
\emph{Obstruction:} multistart at a fixed target resolution $n$ exhausts its basin and
returns the same local optimum. \emph{Mechanism:} resolution is non-monotonic in
optimization --- average-pool a higher-resolution solution down to $n$ (pooling preserves
dominant low-frequency structure where interpolation washes it out) and refine; the
transferred point lands in a structurally distinct basin. On P3 (second autocorrelation) a
$1.6\text{M}\to 100\text{k}$ transfer moved $0.96199\to 0.96221$ and temporarily took rank \#1
at posting (P3 stands at \#3 in the 2026-06-22 snapshot, Table~\ref{tab:standings}).
\src{techniques/cross-resolution-basin-transfer.md}

\paragraph{Dinkelbach fractional programming.}
\emph{Obstruction:} a ratio objective $C=N(f)/D(f)$ is non-convex and, at large $n$,
cutting-plane LP stalls (a solver wall, not a warm-start failure). \emph{Mechanism:} replace
the ratio with a parametric sequence of subtractive subproblems
$\max_f\,[N(f)-\lambda D(f)]$ with $\lambda \leftarrow N(f^\ast)/D(f^\ast)$, a LogSumExp
surrogate for the $L^\infty$ factor, and a $\beta$-cascade for stability. The parametric
reduction is classical~\cite{dinkelbach1967fractional}; here it is the champion method on the
autocorrelation family (P2/P3), replacing a cutting-plane LP~\cite{lee2015cuttingplane,matolcsi2010autoconvolution}
that stalls at large $n$. \src{techniques/dinkelbach-fractional-programming.md,
concepts/fractional-programming-dinkelbach.md}

\paragraph{Warm data-driven self-pruning.}
\emph{Obstruction:} when the optimum has compact support (exactly-zero cells), full-support
optimizers cannot reach it, and an $N$-agent tie looks like a floor. \emph{Mechanism:}
warm-start from a converged full-support solution, iteratively zero the smallest-magnitude
cells (the optimizer chooses which), and re-optimize under $f=v^2$ --- whose curvature
$\varphi''(0)=2$ gives a finite KKT signal at zero, unlike $\exp(v)$, which can never reach
exact zeros. This broke a two-agent tie on P2 and set a verified arena record
($1.5028609\to 1.5028506$). It is the canonical instance of the wall-hit corollary in
\S\ref{sec:harness}: a single untried operator beat a presumed global minimum.
\src{techniques/warm-self-pruning-compact-support.md, findings/p2-warm-self-pruning-beats-record.md}

\paragraph{Parallel-tempering simulated annealing.}
\emph{Obstruction:} a rough, gradient-flat-but-stochastically-improvable landscape on which
greedy descent stalls. \emph{Mechanism:} run 8--64 SA replicas on a geometric temperature
ladder with periodic Metropolis exchange --- hot replicas explore, cold replicas exploit ---
with contribution-weighted sampling and $O(n)$ incremental evaluation. On kissing-number
$d=11$ it beat single-replica greedy by $730\times$. The precondition matters: it fails on a
genuinely single-funneled minimum (Thomson $n=282$), so a cage-structure diagnostic runs
first. \src{techniques/parallel-tempering-sa.md}

\paragraph{mpmath ULP-step coordinate descent.}
\emph{Obstruction:} at the float64 floor, smooth surrogates die --- their kernel noise floor
($\sim 10^{-12}$) sits above the residual loss ($\sim 10^{-13}$), so no $\beta$ helps.
\emph{Mechanism:} discrete $\pm1/\pm2$ ulp single-coordinate descent, evaluating proposed
moves in mpmath at $\mathrm{dps}\ge 50$, gated by a float64 active-pair screen for a
$10\times$ speedup. Drove P6 from $1.97\mathrm{e}{-}13$ to $1.59\mathrm{e}{-}13$ in minutes.
\src{techniques/mpmath-ulp-polish.md}

\paragraph{Boundary-snap for interpolation kinks.}
\emph{Obstruction:} for envelopes with first-order discontinuities at known points, smooth
optimizers avoid the boundary (the gradient is discontinuous there), yet the optimum
requires a sample exactly on it. \emph{Mechanism:} snap the nearest sample to each kink and
re-polish neighbors; iterate snap\,$\to$\,polish\,$\to$\,basin-hop. On P13 each round yielded
$\sim 1.24\mathrm{e}{-}8$, compounding over a few rounds. \src{techniques/boundary-snap-for-kinks.md}

\paragraph{Why these generalize.}
Each technique is indexed in the knowledge base not by the problem it first cracked but by the
\emph{obstruction class} it addresses (basin exhaustion, ratio objectives, compact support,
rough landscapes, the float64 floor, interpolation kinks). That indexing is what lets the
next problem in the same class inherit the method --- the compounding the harness is built to
produce. \src{docs/wiki/techniques/README.md}

Beyond these six, the knowledge base documents the supporting toolbox each problem drew on:
CMA-ES with warm starts~\cite{hansen2016cmaes,nomura2020warmcmaes}, the
game-theoretic-RL search that set the kissing-number frontier~\cite{ma2025kissinggame}, and
the semidefinite-programming bound machinery~\cite{bachoc2006sdp,mittelmann2009sdpkissing,delaat2024kissingsdp,lasserre2010nonnegativity,razborov2010flag}
that supplies the analytical cross-checks triple-verify relies on --- with the LSQR
least-squares solver~\cite{paige1982lsqr} behind the integer-snapping recipe that took the
kissing record past JSAgent's contribution.

\section{Collaboration \emph{and} Competition}
\label{sec:collab}

The platform paper~\cite{bianchi2026einstein} argues, from above, that discovery on the arena
is collective. From inside a single competitor, the same fact has a sharper, more conflicted
texture worth recording.

\paragraph{Competition: the leaderboard as a forcing function.}
A public, strictly-ranked board with an earliest-submission tiebreak is a strong incentive ---
and a blunt one. On P1 three agents hold a byte-identical score; the board orders them purely
by submission time, and JSAgent's three-week-later submission is rank \#2 behind Together-AI
at the same number. The tiebreak rewards speed-to-post over depth-of-result, which pushes
toward early disclosure of partial progress.

\paragraph{Collaboration: results as links in shared lineages.}
Several of JSAgent's strongest results are intermediate, not terminal. On kissing $d=11$ it
iterated on another agent's strong $n=594$ construction within a lineage that other agents
later carried to $604$. On the second autocorrelation inequality its $0.962214$ was the
frontier when posted and was later surpassed ($0.962643$) by another agent working the same
basin-transfer approach, which the platform paper places in one lineage (Fig.~3b). Progress
here was genuinely collective: JSAgent both built on others' public work and had its own built
upon. \src{findings/einstein-arena-paper-jsagent-recognition-2026-06.md}

\paragraph{What was public, and what was not.}
To be precise about the boundary, since it bears on the ``compounding private record'' claim:
JSAgent \emph{read} the arena's public discussion board (that is how it found warm starts) and
\emph{submitted} solutions to the leaderboard. Early in the campaign it also \emph{posted}
selected method writeups to the board (for example, notes on breaking P2 peak-locking via the
square parameterization and on the P18 tolerance structure); the project later adopted a
no-external-posts discipline, after which submissions were its only public output. Throughout,
the full reasoning record --- the knowledge base, cycle logs, and promotion history ---
remained private and canonical during the campaign; there were no blog or social-media posts.
The collaboration was thus asymmetric: JSAgent drew continuously on public information and
contributed verified results (and, early on, some method writeups), but its compounding memory
stayed private.

\paragraph{The tension, from inside.}
These two pulls conflict. The forcing function rewards posting a partial result early; posting
it hands a rival the next rung. This is exactly where this project's stance --- a private,
compounding knowledge base as the canonical record, and no external posts --- sits awkwardly
against an open shared board: the knowledge base keeps the wisdom auditable and undiluted, but
it does not put a flag on the board, and on an earliest-submission board that has a real cost.

We do not resolve the tension; we reframe it. Over a single record it feels like a
zero-sum race, but over a long enough horizon \emph{competition is the most effective form of
collaboration}. A strictly-ranked board compels each entrant to publish a result others can
immediately stand on; the very rivalry that decides one record is the mechanism that drives
the whole field forward, faster than any single agent --- or any politely coordinated group
--- would manage alone. The kissing-number lineage ($582$, unbeaten for $\sim$42 years, then
$592\to593\to594\to604$ in months once the board existed) is the case in point: each agent
competed for the top slot, and in competing handed the next agent a higher floor. On this
arena, competition and collaboration were not in tension but the same engine.
\src{.claude/rules/axioms.md (A2), findings/arena-standings-snapshot-2026-06-22.md}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We situate JSAgent along two axes: where the discovery \emph{state} lives, and what the
agent's durable \emph{artifact} is.

\paragraph{Single-run, search-based discovery agents.}
Program-search and evolutionary systems --- FunSearch~\cite{romeraparedes2023funsearch},
AlphaEvolve~\cite{novikov2025alphaevolve}, ShinkaEvolve~\cite{lange2025shinkaevolve}, and
evolution-meets-RL search~\cite{surina2025algorithmdiscovery} --- and test-time-training
discovery~\cite{yuksekgonul2026tttdiscover} each run as a single orchestrated pipeline. In
these systems the durable artifact is usually the discovered program, construction, or
policy; the reasoning is not exposed as a human-readable, versioned knowledge base. JSAgent
instead treats the reusable, human-readable memory itself as a first-class artifact.

\paragraph{Autonomous research and AI-for-science systems.}
A second line builds end-to-end ``scientist'' agents: The AI
Scientist~\cite{lu2024aiscientist,yamada2025aiscientistv2}, an AI
co-scientist~\cite{gottweis2025coscientist}, Kosmos~\cite{mitchener2025kosmos}, an AI
co-mathematician~\cite{zheng2026comathematician}, and the Virtual
Lab~\cite{swanson2025virtuallab}, with AgentRxiv~\cite{schmidgall2025agentrxiv} adding a shared
preprint substrate. In mathematics specifically, formal-reasoning agents have reached olympiad
level and accelerated Lean proving~\cite{hubert2025olympiad,kung2026leap,yang2024formalmath}.
JSAgent is narrower (one competitor on optimization problems) but keeps a sharper invariant:
every claim passes an objective external verifier, and the reasoning trail is retained.

\paragraph{Shared-state, multi-agent, and self-improving systems.}
The EinsteinArena platform~\cite{bianchi2026einstein,togetherai2026arena} makes the
\emph{platform} the shared persistent state; CORAL~\cite{qu2026coral} and recursive multi-agent
systems~\cite{zou2026recursivemas} pursue multi-agent evolution; and a broad literature
designs, composes, or self-improves agent collectives
(MetaGPT~\cite{hong2023metagpt}, Voyager~\cite{wang2023voyager}, ExpeL~\cite{zhao2023expel},
automated design of agentic systems~\cite{hu2024ads}, agentic context
engineering~\cite{zhang2025ace}), evaluated on benchmarks such as
SWE-bench~\cite{jimenez2023swebench}. These socialize or optimize state \emph{across} agents
or runs. JSAgent's persistence is orthogonal: a single agent's private, compounding memory,
contributed to the shared board only when verified.

\paragraph{Harness-as-artifact.}
Closest in spirit is observability-driven harness evolution~\cite{lin2026ahe} and treating
code as the agent harness~\cite{ning2026codeharness}, which evolve a coding agent's harness
from run telemetry. JSAgent's third axis is distinct: a \emph{persistent, human-readable
personal memory} --- a knowledge base of concepts, findings, and plain-language behavioral
rules --- that the agent both writes and is governed by, and that a human reads to steer. The
artifact is legible by design, because legibility is what makes the human's problem-selection
role (\S\ref{sec:discussion}) possible.

\paragraph{Math-frontier context.}
The constructions and proven bounds for the arena's problems are catalogued by the operator's
reference work~\cite{georgiev2025exploration}, which the knowledge base cites throughout for
SOTA values and the analytical bounds that triple-verify checks against. The ceiling of
autonomous mathematics was raised dramatically when an LLM produced a research-level disproof
of Erd{\H o}s's 1946 unit-distance conjecture~\cite{openai2026unitdistance}; our contribution is
not a single dramatic result but a \emph{method} for accumulating many checkable ones.

\section{Are Agents Ready for Real Scientific Contribution?}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{The case for.}
The submitted constructions were each verified three independent ways (triple-verify) and
posted against hardened public verifiers anyone can re-run; the standings themselves
(Table~\ref{tab:standings}) are a timestamped public sweep (\S\ref{sec:eval}), and the trail
that produced them --- every question, every dead-end, every cycle --- is auditable in the
knowledge base and the cycle log. On this evidence, in domains with cheap objective
verifiers, agents are already capable of genuine, checkable contribution.

\paragraph{The honest case against, and the limits.}
Several of the results are empirical ties or near-ties rather than proofs: no dual
certificate yet matches a value to confirm global optimality, so an $N$-agent tie should be
read as a sharp shared basin, not a theorem --- the gold standard remains an exact proof, as
in Viazovska's solution of sphere packing in dimension~8~\cite{viazovska2016spherepacking}. Human gates remain load-bearing throughout
(problem selection, ingest approval, promotion). And the methodology has only been
exercised where an \emph{objective verifier} exists.

\paragraph{The human role, operationalized.}
The workflow was agent-primary (Table~\ref{tab:roles}): the agent produced the code, scripts,
knowledge-base pages, and manuscript drafts; what did \emph{not} automate was \emph{selecting}
what to work on, distrusting a ``floor'' claim, and judging when a result was worth the
credibility cost of a submission. The agent is a strong executor and a weak selector of what
matters; the human supplies direction, and the precondition for supplying it is comprehension
--- the knowledge base and harness exist partly to keep that comprehension tractable.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Human--agent division of labor. ``Automated gate'' = a deterministic check that
already enforces part of the step.}
\label{tab:roles}
\begin{tabular}{llll}

\paragraph{Macro-management and unattended operation.}
An important operating mode was long, unattended runs --- often multi-hour and overnight ---
in which the human supplied a macro-objective and the agent executed search, verification, and
logging cycles without step-by-step supervision. This mode tested whether the harness could
preserve direction on its own. It is also the practical bridge to autonomy: if an agent makes
real progress unattended under a long-term goal, it may eventually reach results a human
operator could not have produced alone. We offer the records here as early, modest evidence
consistent with that possibility, not as proof of it.

\paragraph{The trajectory toward autonomy.}
We expect this division of labor to narrow. As the evaluation and the harness themselves
become automated --- the agent proposing its own questions, judging its own results against
objective verifiers, and rewriting its own rules from the cycle log --- the loop can in
principle close and self-improve without a human in it. The gating factor is
\emph{verification}. Mathematics is the easy case precisely because correctness is
objective and cheap to check: a verifier either accepts a construction or it does not.
Domains like biology and the natural sciences are harder not because the search is harder
but because there is no comparably objective, fast oracle for ``is this right'' --- ground
truth requires experiment, time, and judgment. Autonomy will therefore arrive
domain-by-domain, in order of how objective and how cheap their verification is; math
first, and open-ended empirical science last.

\paragraph{The central caveat, stated plainly.}
This work was \emph{progressive, experimental, and exploratory} --- a real campaign run over
many cycles --- not a controlled experiment. Its evidence that a compounding knowledge base
\emph{causes} better outcomes is therefore observational, not causal. The clean test is an
explicit A/B ablation: hold a model and compute budget fixed, and on held-out problems compare
condition~A, the agent solving from its own reasoning with an empty knowledge base, against
condition~B, the same agent with the full compounding knowledge base, measuring the gap in best
verified score at fixed budget (and time-to-result). We have designed this test but not run it;
it is the most important next step, and until it is run the causal reading of our traces should
be treated as a hypothesis the observational evidence supports but does not establish.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Wisdom compounds; rank is a byproduct. JSAgent's standing on EinsteinArena --- the highest
rank-\#1 and top-3 counts of any agent, as of 2026-06-22 --- is real and independently
verifiable, but it is downstream of the thing this paper is actually about: a knowledge base
and a set of behavioral rules that grew, cycle by cycle, into an agent that gets smarter from
its own recorded failures. The artifact is the knowledge base, the agent, and this paper that
distills them. The open frontier is autonomy --- closing the loop so the agent proposes and
judges its own questions --- and its gate is verification, which math offers cheaply and the
natural sciences do not yet.

\section{A Personal Account}
\label{app:personal}
\emph{This appendix is the first-person story of how the work began and what it felt like to
do. It is deliberately separated from the methodology above; nothing in the contribution
depends on it.}

\paragraph{An accidental start.}
I am not a mathematician by training. The problems looked hard enough that at first I did not
attempt them at all; what changed was a free weekend and the impulse to just try one. My
background is in physics, so I began with the problem I felt I could actually read --- the
uncertainty principle (P9), built on the same inequality that bounds position and momentum in
quantum mechanics. After a good deal of trial and error, it eventually worked. That a
non-specialist could enter at all, and that the first problem I dared to try would end up
among the agent's strongest results, is itself part of what this paper is about: the agent
supplied the mathematical execution; I supplied a direction I understood well enough to steer.

\paragraph{The first discipline: trust nothing unverified.}
At the start I wanted to top the leaderboard, and I pushed hard to break records directly. My
very first submission was wrong --- a score I trusted locally did not hold up against the
arena's verifier. That failure set the project's first discipline: rather than trust a single
number, I had the agent build a local \emph{triple verifier} --- a fast approximate check to
drive the optimization loop, backed by a slower exact reimplementation and a third
independent cross-check --- so that no result was believed until it agreed with itself more
than one way (\S\ref{sec:harness}).

\paragraph{Cold start, warm start, and when to start over.}
Building each solution from a \emph{cold start} --- from scratch --- proved difficult. So I
let the agent read what other competitors were discussing on the public board, and found that
\emph{warm-starting} from others' posted solutions was not only permitted but encouraged.
That reframed the work: discovery is collaborative, and taking one real step beyond someone
else's contribution is itself a contribution --- often a far more efficient route than
starting over. But warm-starting has a failure mode. Building on an existing solution can
lock you into its basin, and past a point the only way to a genuinely better result is to
abandon it and restart from a very different configuration. The kissing-number problem in
dimension~11 was the clearest illustration: a strong arrangement of $594$ vectors ---
contributed by \texttt{alpha\_omega\_agents} and built on by much of the field, this agent
included --- anchored a long run of incremental gains, yet pushing the record further (to
$604$) required a structurally different construction, a restart rather than a refinement.

\paragraph{Problems are related --- so build a memory.}
As I let the agent work more problems, a pattern surfaced: many were related. The first,
second, and third autocorrelation inequalities are a family; a method that cracked one --- a
parameterization, a refinement step --- often carried to the next. That observation became a
design decision: build a shared knowledge base so that what was learned on one problem could
be transferred \emph{horizontally} to others, instead of being rediscovered each time. And
because failure is as informative as success --- a dead-end, recorded with its reason, is a
durable way of knowing what does \emph{not} work --- I kept those too, not only the wins.

\paragraph{The needle moves.}
The needle moved gradually and then suddenly. Early submissions were unremarkable; the
turning points came when failures started being written down \emph{with their reasons}, and
later runs began standing on those write-ups instead of rediscovering the same walls. The
clearest instance --- a verified arena record produced verbatim from a four-day-old
dead-end's ``what might still work'' note --- convinced me the deliverable was never the
score. It was the page that produced the score.

\paragraph{Plan first; then let the machine enforce the plan.}
Every campaign began with planning, not code: a detailed plan document stating, in writing,
exactly what to do and why before any optimizer ran. At the outset this was a \emph{manual}
process --- I wrote and curated the plans, and the self-improvement steps happened because I
asked for them. The arc of the project was to make that discipline progressively
self-driving, culminating in the meta-learning harness loop of \S\ref{sec:harness}. The
shorthand for why: discretion decays, gates compound.

\paragraph{Macro-management, and a conviction.}
The hardest progress came not from interactive back-and-forth but from long, unattended runs,
frequently overnight, managed at the macro level: set a long-term objective, encourage
persistence in plain terms, and let the agent run while I slept. The conviction underneath it
is simple: if an agent is truly remarkable, it should make real progress unattended under a
long-term goal --- and if it can do that, it should eventually solve problems I cannot. This
paper is, in part, a test of that conviction.

\paragraph{What the workflow suggests about the research role.}
Working this way changed what I think the job of a researcher is becoming. I wrote almost
none of this by hand, and yet I understood every step well enough to redirect it --- and that
understanding, not the typing, was the contribution. If the measure of readiness is whether
an agent can produce checkable results on open problems, the evidence here says yes. If the
measure is whether a person can still steer that work toward what matters and vouch for it,
the answer is also yes --- and the two are not in tension. They are the division of labor I
expect to define the next several years of doing science with agents.
