# Kolountzakis–Matolcsi LP bound (autoconvolution-supremum framework)

## TL;DR

The **Kolountzakis–Matolcsi framework** (Kolountzakis 1996; Matolcsi–Vinuesa 2010) is the LP-relaxation machinery underlying every modern numerical bound on `inf_{f ≥ 0, ∫f = 1, supp f ⊆ [-1/4, 1/4]} ‖f * f‖_∞` — the autoconvolution-supremum problem that arena P2 (and indirectly P3, P4) discretizes. Discretize `f` into `n` step-function bins, write the supremum as a linear program over `n` densities + slack, and a **cutting-plane LP** descends rapidly to a tight basin at small `n`. The framework is *exact in the limit* (n → ∞ recovers the true infimum) but suffers a brutal solver-scaling wall around `n ≈ 10⁴`. Below the wall it's the cheapest path to a competitive score; above the wall, switch to away-step / pairwise Frank–Wolfe.

## What it is

For a non-negative `f : [−1/4, 1/4] → ℝ_{≥0}` with `∫f = 1`, the autoconvolution `(f * f)(x)` is supported on `[−1/2, 1/2]`. The arena P2 quantity is

```
S  =  inf  ‖f * f‖_∞   (over admissible f).
```

**Discretization**. Partition `[−1/4, 1/4]` into `n` equal bins of width `h = 1/(2n)`. Replace `f` with a piecewise-constant `f_n = Σᵢ vᵢ · 1_{Bᵢ}` where `vᵢ ≥ 0`. The convolution `f_n * f_n` is then piecewise-linear on `[−1/2, 1/2]` with kinks at every `i·h`. Writing `s = sup (f_n * f_n)`, the discretized problem is the LP

```
minimize  s
subject to  Σⱼ vⱼ · vⱼ₊ₖ · h  ≤  s     for every k ∈ {0, 1, …, n}   (*)
            Σᵢ vᵢ · h  =  1
            vᵢ ≥ 0.
```

The constraints (*) are **bilinear in `v`** so this isn't directly an LP. The K–M relaxation linearizes by introducing the **autocorrelation vector** `aₖ = Σⱼ vⱼ · vⱼ₊ₖ` and writing the problem as an LP in `(v, a, s)` with the bilinear coupling enforced via cutting planes:

```
minimize  s
subject to  aₖ · h  ≤  s            for every k
            Σᵢ vᵢ · h  =  1
            vᵢ ≥ 0
            (cutting planes:  aₖ ≥ 2 v* · v - constant   from violated bilinear couplings)
```

Each cut is generated by computing the most-violated bilinear constraint at the current `v` iterate and adding a tangent-plane separator. This is exactly the **Cohn LP framework** as adapted to autoconvolution.

## Why it matters

1. **Disproof of Schinzel–Schmidt**. Matolcsi–Vinuesa 2010 used the framework to construct an explicit asymmetric step-function `f` that nearly saturates a Hardy–Hilbert-style bound, *disproving* the long-standing conjecture that the extremal `f` must be the indicator of an interval. The disproof opened the door to all subsequent piecewise-constant work (Cloninger–Steinerberger 2017, Boyer–Li 2025, Jaech–Joseph 2025, Rechnitzer 2026).
2. **Numerical lower bounds for `S`**. Each finite-`n` LP solve gives a rigorous lower bound on `S` (since adding constraints can only relax the LP). The 2010 paper hit `S ≥ 1.262`; by 2025–26 the framework had been pushed to `S ≥ 0.901564` (Boyer–Li, n = 575) and `S ≥ 0.901564 + 1e-6` (Rechnitzer, 128-digit interval arithmetic).
3. **B_h[g] / Sidon-set duality**. The autoconvolution-supremum problem is dual to the maximum-density problem for `B_2[g]`-sets (continuous Sidon sets). Vinuesa's 2010 PhD thesis and Cloninger–Steinerberger 2017 articulate this duality; P19's difference-basis problem sits in the same family.

## When it applies in the arena

| Problem | LP framework | Status | Dominant alternative |
|---|---|---|---|
| **P2 First Autocorrelation** | central tool at small/medium `n` | rapid descent at `n ≤ 200`; stalls at `n ≥ 10⁴` from SOTA warmstart | v² parameterization + smooth-max L-BFGS at n = 90k |
| **P3 Higher-Order Autocorrelation** | extends naturally | solver-bound at the n required for competitive bounds | Dinkelbach fractional programming + cascade |
| **P4 Closed-Form Autocorrelation** | provides numerical companion to closed-form attempts | analytic conjecture not yet decisive | analytical equioscillation theory |
| **P19 Difference Bases** | conceptual bridge via B_h[g] duality | not directly applied — Kronecker decomposition dominates | `A ⊕ λ·R` Kronecker construction |

## How it's actually used

The wiki's **`techniques/dinkelbach-fractional-programming.md`** wraps the K–M LP inside a fractional-programming outer loop for ratio objectives (P3 / P4). The `findings/lp-solver-scaling.md` (lessons #47, #57, #95) document the practical envelope:

- **Vanilla cutting-plane LP** descends rapidly at `n ≤ 200` (random init `2.04 → 1.5187` in 20 iters via sparse HiGHS).
- **Warm-start from SOTA at `n = 30 000`** sees a descent direction in round 1 (`s* = 1.54 < 1.6`-ish SOTA), but CLARABEL interior-point bogs down past 5 min/round at memory > 7 GB. `scipy.linprog highs-ipm` and `highs-ds` never complete round 1 at `n ≥ 2000` in 3–8 min.
- **Beyond `n ≈ 10⁴` from SOTA**, vanilla Frank–Wolfe (the LP's first-order cousin) lands on the SOTA face and stays — it's a true LP fixed point. The documented exit is **away-step / pairwise Frank–Wolfe** (Lacoste-Julien & Jaggi 2015), which provably escapes face-of-polytope traps.
- **Float64 ceiling at `n ≥ 100k`**: convolution accumulation noise saturates around `1e-16`. Final polish requires either Modal A100 `torch.fft.rfft` at float64, or 128-digit interval arithmetic à la Rechnitzer 2026.

The practical cross-over: K–M LP wins below `n = 1000` (where it solves cheaply and reaches the resolution ceiling above SOTA — i.e., the LP is *resolution-limited*, not solver-limited), and loses above `n = 10 000` (where solver-scaling beats descent quality). The dead zone in between is the regime FOSS solvers stall.

## Why the SOTA stalls the LP

The K–M LP at competitive `n` is dual to a **face of the simplex of admissible step-function densities**. The current SOTA configuration sits *exactly on a vanilla-LP fixed point*: the cutting-plane procedure can identify violated constraints, but the LP optimum at the next iterate stays on the same face. This is structurally identical to the "vanilla Frank–Wolfe lands on the face and stays" failure mode — and the same fix applies.

The fix (per `findings/lp-solver-scaling.md` lesson #47): **away-step Frank–Wolfe** explicitly steps *off* the active face whenever the linearization minimum sits at a face-vertex with positive multiplier; **pairwise Frank–Wolfe** swaps weight between two active vertices simultaneously, also escaping the face. Both are documented in [`techniques/frank-wolfe.md`](../techniques/frank-wolfe.md) (away-step / pairwise variants).

## Pitfalls

- **Resolution ceiling masquerading as descent**. At small `n` (≤ 1000) the LP descends to a basin floor that is *above* SOTA — the resolution is too coarse to resolve sub-ulp differences. Lifting `n` doesn't help once the solver stalls. Easy to mistake "LP converged at 1.504" for "true bound is 1.504" when the truth is "LP at this `n` cannot resolve below 1.504."
- **CLARABEL/IPM RAM blow-up**. Cutting-plane LP at `n ≥ 30 000` accumulates constraints until matrix factorizations exhaust 8 GB. MOSEK/Gurobi commercial solvers handle this regime; FOSS solvers do not.
- **Symmetry trap**. The original Schinzel–Schmidt conjecture (extremal `f` is `1_I`) is *false* — but the LP, with symmetric initialization, can spend many iterations near a symmetric local-optimum that's worse than the true asymmetric extremum. Random asymmetric warmstart, or explicit symmetry-breaking constraints, fixes this. The Matolcsi–Vinuesa counterexample is the canonical reference.
- **Bilinear coupling drift**. With finite cutting-plane budget, `aₖ` may not match `Σⱼ vⱼ vⱼ₊ₖ` exactly at the LP optimum — only up to the cuts added so far. Always verify the final `(v, a)` pair satisfies the bilinear coupling within tolerance before reporting the bound.

## Pedagogical bridge to other LP bounds

The K–M LP is structurally a **special case of Cohn-style LP duality** (cf. [`cohn-elkies-bound`](cohn-elkies-bound.md)) — Cohn–Elkies asks for a Fourier-positive function bounding sphere-packing density; K–M asks for a step-function bounding autoconvolution supremum. Both rely on:

1. A non-negativity constraint (Fourier-positive in CE; pointwise-positive in K–M).
2. An integral normalization (`f̂(0) = f(0)` in CE; `∫f = 1` in K–M).
3. An LP relaxation that's *exact* under a magic function (Viazovska-style modular forms in CE; not yet known for K–M — open problem).

The pedagogical lesson: K–M is the **autoconvolution-shaped sibling** of Cohn–Elkies. If a Viazovska-style magic function exists for autoconvolution (open since 1996), it would saturate the K–M bound the way Viazovska 2016 saturated Cohn–Elkies for `d = 8`. No such function is currently known.
