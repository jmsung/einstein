# ON LEV’S PERIODICITY CONJECTURE

CHRISTIAN REIHER

Abstract. We classify the sum-free subsets of Fn3 whose density exceeds 61. This yields a resolution of Vsevolod Lev’s periodicity conjecture, which asserts that if a sum-free sub-

![image 1](<2024-reiher-lev-periodicity-conjecture_images/imageFile1.png>)

set A Ď Fn3 is maximal with respect to inclusion and aperiodic (in the sense that there is no non-zero vector v satisfying A ` v “ A), then |A| ď 21p3n´1 ` 1q —a bound known to be optimal if n ‰ 2, while for n “ 2 there are no such sets.

§1 Introduction

A subset A of an abelian group G is said to be sum-free if the equation x ` y “ z has no solutions with x,y,z P A. Thus Fermat’s last theorem is equivalent to the statement that for every k ě 3 the set of positive kth powers is sum-free; this motivated Schur [15] to prove his famous theorem that the set of positive integers cannot be represented as a union of ﬁnitely many sum-free sets. Since the 1960’s the study of sum-free sets has intensiﬁed due to the problems and results of Erdős (see e.g. [4]). We refer to [3,5] for some recent milestones in the area and to [12] for a survey.

When the ambient group is a binary vector space G “ Fn2, sum-free sets have connections to projective geometry and coding theory. It is easily seen that in this case the sum-free sets of largest cardinality are hyperplanes not passing through the origin. Davydov and Tombak [2] obtained the much deeper result that sum-free sets which cannot be covered by a hyperplane have size at most 5 ¨ 2n´4. They also have a stronger theorem on maximal sum-free sets in Fn2, i.e., sum-free sets that are maximal with respect to inclusion. Notably they showed that every such set A possessing more than 2n´2 ` 1 elements is periodic, which means that there is a non-zero vector v such that A ` v “ A.

Vsevolod Lev [8, 9] initiated the study of similar questions in other ﬁnite vector spaces. Following the title of [8] we call vector spaces over the three-element ﬁeld F3 ternary. By an early observation of Wallis, Street, and Wallis [17, Corollary 7.11] sum-free subsets of Fn3 have at most the size 3n´1, the extremal examples again being hyperplanes not passing through the origin. The main result of [8] asserts that if a sum-free subset of Fn3 has more than 5 ¨ 3n´3 elements, then it can be covered by a hyperplane; this bound is indeed optimal

Despite being included as Problem 7.10 into the well-known problem collection of Croot and Lev [1], the conjecture has remained open for almost two decades.

- Lemma 1.3. Every primitive subset A of a ﬁnite ternary vector space V is maximal sum-free and satisﬁes |A| “ 16p|V | ` 3|SympAq|q.

Theorem 1.4. A subset A of a ﬁnite ternary vector space V with |A| ą 61|V | is maximal sum-free if and only if it is primitive.

§3 Inductive arguments Let us recapitulate a fundamental result of additive combinatorics due to Kneser [6,7].

Theorem 3.1 (Kneser). If A, B are two ﬁnite nonempty subsets of an abelian group G and K “ SympA ` Bq, then

|A ` B| ě |A ` K| ` |B ` K| ´ |K|.

If G is ﬁnite, then |G| and the right side are divisible by |K|; this immediately implies the following well-known fact. Corollary 3.2. If two subsets A, B of a ﬁnite abelian group G satisfy |A| ` |B| ą |G|, then A ` B “ G.

Our other applications of Kneser’s theorem factorise through the following statement.

- Lemma 3.3. Let A, B, C be three subsets of a ﬁnite abelian group G such that A ` B is disjoint to C. If 2|A| ` 2|B| ` |C| ą 2|G| and C ‰ ∅, then there is a subgroup K of G such that |A ` K| ` |B ` K| “ |G| and C is contained in a single coset of K.


Proof. Notice that A “ B “ ∅ would yield the contradiction |C| ą 2|G|. If exactly one of A and B is empty, we can simply take K “ G. So we may assume from now on that A and B are nonempty. It will eventually turn out that K “ SympA ` Bq is as required. Since A ` B is a union of cosets of K, it is disjoint to C ` K and, therefore, Kneser’s theorem yields

|G| ě |A ` B| ` |C ` K| ě |A ` K| ` |B ` K| ` |C ` K| ´ |K|, (3.1)

whence

|G| ě |A ` K| ` |B ` K|. (3.2) Furthermore, (3.1) entails

2|A ` K| ` 2|B ` K| ` |C ` K| ě 2|A| ` 2|B| ` |C| ą 2|G| ě |G| ` |A ` K| ` |B ` K| ` |C ` K| ´ |K|,

i.e., |A ` K| ` |B ` K| ą |G| ´ |K|. As both sides are multiples of |K|, this shows in combination with (3.2) that |A ` K| ` |B ` K| “ |G|. In view of (3.1) we can conclude

- |C ` K| ď |K| and, consequently, C is indeed contained in a single coset of K.
